In his 2005 article "Where the Counterculture Met the New Economy," historian Fred Turner identifies two distinct political traditions that emerged from 1960s America. The New Left believed social change required collective political struggle — organizing, protest, coalition-building, and structural reform of existing institutions. The New Communalists (associated with Stewart Brand and the Whole Earth Catalog) believed the real obstacle was consciousness itself: by giving individuals better tools and information, society could be transformed from the inside out, without direct political confrontation. Turner's insight is that these two traditions had entirely different weight matrices — the same social problem, fed through each ideology, would be routed along different value pathways and emerge as radically different prescriptions for action. This simulation renders that difference as a literal neural network: two architectures, same inputs, visibly different weights, divergent outputs.
